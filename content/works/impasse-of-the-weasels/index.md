## Program Notes

*Impasse of the Weasels* originates from the impressions I gathered during a journey in January 2025 with my parents and grandmother in Northeast China (so-called *Manchuria* or *[Manchukuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchukuo)*.) At the time it was a deep, tranquil winter and an expanse of snow covered almost everything visible, which reminds me of the writer <ruby>[Chi Zijian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chi_Zijian)<rt>遲子建</rt></ruby>, who has written in this region many works infused with magic (the word “shaman” comes from [Tungusic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungusic_languages), spoken in Northeast China and East Siberia) and sentiments of dramatic bitterness, but at the same time every word she wrote is veiled in such a grand and white kind of silence. This style and atmosphere inspired me of how I set up the tone for this composition.

The Evenki flag is like an inverted Estonian *[Sinimustvalge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Estonia)*, where *sini-* (blue) happens to be a conjugation of *Sina* (China), and the white part happens to be the Japanese flag because of the red sun. It seems that the history itself has prefigured this denouement that Northeast China, having a North Baltic temperament, was compelled to fight against Japanese aggression but has been ultimately unable to efface its socio-cultural impact. In the desolate rust-belt scenery, I was also able to see traces of the psychoses in the history, from the impact of ancient occultism to the mental disintegration, perhaps hereditary, of <ruby>[Wanrong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empress_Wanrong)<rt>婉容</rt></ruby>, the last empress who was forced back to her native land. These became the cultural and emotional inspirations for the material of this composition.

As someone raised in the subtropics, I was acutely aware of the cold air stimulating my under-developed sinuses and insufficient amount of capillaries, which forced my attention toward the often overlooked mechanism of breath and the remarkable musical and emotional richness it contains. Another layer of the mechanism of breath (and olfaction) that I have been putting thoughts into is its mnemonic function. The Japanese writer <ruby>[Takayuki Kiyooka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Takayuki_Kiyooka)<rt>清岡卓行</rt></ruby>, in <ruby>*[Acacia of Dairen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalian_of_Acasia_Flowers)*<rt>アカシヤの大連</rt></ruby>, begins the ethnographic remembrance of his childhood in Northeast China through the qualities of breath,

> *The train goes north through Joseon (Korea), crosses the Amnok (Yalu, “duck-green”) River, and finally enters Manchuria. After Andong (the old name of Dandong), Fengcheng isn’t far. It is vast darkness outside, yet limpid, dry air comes in through the window. He breathes this air carelessly and catches that almost-forgotten odor of Manchuria. He thinks the air in Japan is a bit wetter, and the air in Korea drier, but both are plant-based.*

Gradually, in the Northeast cities within the ashen daylight that seemed suspended in the early millennium, I came to recognize a feeling that is not just oblivion, namely the loss of narrative, but the loss of history. During this road trip, I gained what might be called a curious experience of retrograde *ekklēsis* (evocation) having the privilege as a newcomer: through my prior reading of the ethnograph, I was able to acquire illusory and vaporous pointers of “memory,” while the olfactory and physical attributes of the air I breathed when I actually arrived entelechized those pointers. This time-reversal cartographic play of the anamnestic property of breath became the form of this composition.

The title is an allusion to Stefania Pandolfo’s 1997 book *[Impasse of the Angels](https://books.google.com/books/about/Impasse_of_the_Angels.html?id=XnUt6akQeFQC)*, a Maghreb ethnograph of southern Morocco. The phrase “impasse of the angels” is a poetic imagination of *[Barzakh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barzakh)*, the Islamic counterpart of limbo or bardo, at the decline of folk and mythological traditions against post-colonial modernity. It reminds me of the Mongolian director Gu Tao’s 2011 documentary <ruby>*[Shen Yi](https://www.orientindiefilms.com/content/documentary/the-opaque-god/)*<rt>神翳</rt></ruby>, where <ruby>*shen*<rt>神</rt></ruby> means god or deity and <ruby>*yi*<rt>翳</rt></ruby> means blindfold or obscure, bearing some resemblance to Pandolfo’s title. The documentary tells a story of an old [Oroqen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oroqen_people) shaman who failed to find a successor of her tradition in the century where no one believes in it anymore. In Northeast China, Siberian weasels are considered spiritual and are worshipped, and I thought it would be a nice replacement for the angels.
