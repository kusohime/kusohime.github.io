## Abstract

This article discusses, from the standpoint of the anthropology of music, various aspects of deaf people's participation/non-participation in musical practices, taking Bali, Indonesia, as its case. It first surveys two genealogies within the anthropology of music. One is acoustic-body theory, which focuses on whole-body resonance, and the other is *musicking* theory, which develops a view of music as an interactive event filled with relationality. It shows that, while both have dismantled the “sound”-centeredness of musical performance, they have also shared a form of relational thinking that places value on participants in musical practices “melting together and becoming one.” Viewed through these theoretical perspectives, local deaf people appear to participate with hearing people in *musicking* interactions as mutually resonating bodies and thereby to connect with others. On this basis, the article examines a deaf child who did not enter the hearing children's dance-play and later danced entirely alone, asking where the “solitude” faced by deaf people in musical settings is located. It finds one part of the cause in the tacit division of social domains within the village community and in problems of language use surrounding the sign language that produces this division. At the same time, it attends to the “self-sufficiency” of the experience of dancing alone. Finally, from a post-relational perspective, the article argues that “all bodies that make music” each possess a fragile, self-sufficient aspect of aloneness.

# I. Is Music Something Heard with the Ears?

## 1. Introduction

When I say, “I study deaf people's[^1] participation in music,” people sometimes react with surprise and a puzzled look. “Deaf people cannot hear with their ears. If music is something heard with the ears, how could deaf people participate in music?” From the standpoint of contemporary Japanese “common sense,” this is an entirely reasonable response. As long as this premise is accepted, music as a form of artistic activity can obstruct the participation of deaf people as a particular group. But is this general “common sense” really correct?

As I will argue, “being deaf” does not in itself mean “being excluded from music.” Deaf people engage with musical practice in various ways. Yet they do not participate in music under exactly the same conditions as hearing people. In what ways do they participate in music, and in what ways do they not participate, or find themselves unable to do so? By describing deaf residents' involvement with music in a community on the Indonesian island of Bali known as the “deaf village,” this article seeks to clarify some of the conditions under which deaf people participate or do not participate in music.

The article is organized as follows. Chapter I reviews how anthropological studies of music have dismantled music's sound-centricity. It traces two major approaches, the genealogy of acoustic body theory and the genealogy of *musicking* theory, which treats music as interaction. I point out that both are ideologically accompanied by relational thinking that makes “melting together and becoming one” music's highest goal. Building on this theoretical review, Chapter II draws on field data from Bali to examine how deaf people participate in hearing people's musical practices and are “connected” to others, considering this participation from the perspectives of acoustic body theory and *musicking* theory. Chapter III turns to a deaf child who did not join hearing children's dance-play and later danced alone. It asks where the “solitude” that deaf people confront in musical settings is located. I argue that this solitude arises from tacit differences between the social domains of hearing and deaf people in the village community, together with issues of language use surrounding sign language. I also discuss the experience of dancing alone through the concept of “self-sufficiency.” The conclusion argues from a post-relational perspective that “all bodies that make music” possess a fragile, self-sufficient aspect of aloneness.

This chapter begins by surveying how the anthropology of music has dismantled the “common sense” introduced above, that “music is something heard with the ears.” It focuses on two genealogies in particular. The first is the acoustic body theory developed by Yoichi Yamada and others, which attends to the multisensory resonance of musical practice. The second is *musicking* theory, which attends to interaction in the “here and now” of sites of musical practice.

## 2. Acoustic-Body Theory: “Resonance” as Whole-Body Sensation

One of the most widely accepted definitions of music in the anthropology of music and ethnomusicology is probably John Blacking's 1973 formulation, “humanly organized sound” [Blacking 1978: 11-12]. Many practices around the world do not fit the Western concept of *music*, including its Japanese translation *ongaku*. Yet if “music” is understood as some form of creative “sound,” it becomes possible to compare the things called “music” in different parts of the world. From this perspective, the idea that music is sound, or something centered on sound, does indeed seem to be an unwavering point of departure for the anthropology of music. The anthropology of music, however, has not treated this “sound” simply as an auditory signal. It has expanded the concept to include the whole body's perception of vibration. In the anthropology of music in Japan, Yoichi Yamada in particular opened up this perspective.

Yamada writes that “‘hearing’ sound with the ears is only a small part of ‘feeling’ sound with the body” [Yamada 2017: 22]. He proposes an “acoustic body theory” to discuss how the body relates to a whole-body form of sound, one that “resounds within a body in which various somatic senses intersect and intermingle” [Yamada 2017: 15]. Drawing on research into bamboo-flute performance and collective singing among the Waxei people of Papua New Guinea, Yamada calls bodies that sense one another through resonance “acoustic bodies” [Yamada 1991, 2000, 2017].

Yamada's acoustic body theory developed against the background of acoustic-ecological works from the 1970s and 1980s, including R. Murray Schafer's soundscape theory [Schafer 2006] and Steven Feld's acoustemology [Feld 1982; Feld 2008]. They are known for advancing an auditory-centered position against the visualism of Western modernity, but neither limits “sound” to auditory stimuli heard by the ears [Feld 2008; Tanaka 2019]. Yamada, who has maintained an intellectual exchange with Feld, places auditory sound at the center of his discussion while continuing to explore sound as something more than what the ears hear. Such sound presses upon the body as a whole-body sensation and connects with the perception of the world. Resonance enters the body, reverberates within it, and resonates with other bodies. For Yamada, this is “the core of musical experience” [Mathieu 1996: 73, via Yamada 2017: 248]. Sound, then, is not so much “sound” as precisely “resonance.”[^2]

Connecting this multisensory approach to sound with Tim Ingold's theory of perception brings us closer to deaf people's musical experience. Ingold criticizes work in the “anthropology of the senses” that presupposes differences among the so-called five senses, such as sight and hearing. In its place, he proposes an account of perception through all the senses, understood as an intermingled whole [Ingold 2000]. He quotes John Hull, who became blind later in life. “We are not a cake with a slice missing. We are a smaller cake” [Hull 1997: xii, via Ingold 2000: 270]. The experience of “blindness,” in other words, does not consist of simply removing the part called “sight” from the perception of a “nondisabled person.” It is experienced as an entirely different perceptual world. The same can be said of deaf people. If sound is “resonance = vibration” felt not only through hearing but through the whole body, including the skin and internal organs, then the resonance perceived by a “hearing-impaired person” is not a partial perception left after something has been subtracted from that of a “nondisabled person.” A deaf person's experience of resonance emerges as a complex perceptual world entirely different from that of a hearing person.

Once “sound” is expanded into this form of whole-body “resonance,” the sound at the core of music's definition can become a resource perceptible to deaf people as well. While tacitly presupposing this acoustic body conception, the anthropology of music has also freed music from auditory centrality through another approach. This is *musicking* theory, which treats musical performance as interaction and attends to its “here and now.”

## 3. *Musicking* Theory: Music as Interaction

“*Musicking*” is a concept proposed by the musicologist Christopher Small in his 1998 book *Musicking: The Meanings of Performing and Listening* [Small 2011]. As the gerund ending “-ing” suggests, *musicking* refers to music as an “event” rather than a “thing.” It is music understood as an occurrence that includes every practice connected with a performance, such as cleaning the venue and taking tickets. The term is catchy and readily understood, so its gerund form tends to receive particular emphasis. Yet the originality of Small's concept lies in its synchronic and relational perspective on musical practice. The idea of turning *music* into a gerund and emphasizing its character as an event had already been proposed three years earlier by David Elliott under the name “*musicing*” [Elliott 1995]. Chihiro Nishijima, one of the Japanese translators of *Musicking*, compares Elliott's *musicing* with Small's *musicking*. Both reject the treatment of music as a thing. Elliott, however, focuses on an individual's learning over time and locates the essence of music in “self-growth, self-knowledge, and (Csikszentmihalyi's) flow.” Small locates it in “the exploration, affirmation, and celebration of group values,” treating “making music” as the construction of collective relations in the moment of the “here and now.” On this point, the two differ substantially [Nishijima 2020: 16-17, commas as in the original]. Small states explicitly that “the act of *musicking* is achieved within a series of ‘relations,’ and the meaning of the act lies within those multiple relations” [Small 2011: 38]. He can thus be said to have established a direction in music research that attends to every relation within musical performance as an event.

How, then, has musical practice as an event been described and analyzed in concrete terms? Researchers have relied on theories of interaction.

Toyoichi Nozawa [2010], another Japanese translator of *Musicking*, draws on Erving Goffman's framework to analyze how the “shout” in an African American Pentecostal church in the United States emerges and gathers intensity. The shout is a trance-state dance accompanied by music. Nozawa [2010] calls the process by which an individual's immersion in trance is elicited through the “involvement” of surrounding worshippers, the band's music, pastors, and others an “immersion-involvement” relation. As what Goffman calls a “focused gathering,” he argues, it gives participants “euphoria,” “fun,” and “secure immersion” [Goffman 1985: 33-34, via Nozawa 2010: 431]. Nozawa especially emphasizes that these experiences arise through nonverbal “bodily and unconscious metamessages” [Nozawa 2010: 432], including bodily contact, calls, and feet stamping or hands clapping in time with the music. In a more recent analysis, he argues that the congregation's “buzz,” an auditory and visual form of noise, is indispensable to the shout [Nozawa 2021]. This work offers a perspective on musical performance as an event of interaction that cannot be reduced to the transmission and reception of linguistic messages.[^3]

In *musicking* theory, music is not a “thing” made of sound that ought to be heard with the ears. It is a distinctive event of interaction that arises in the “here and now” through an entire field of relations while incorporating many bodily, nonverbal signs beyond sound. Small includes the work of ticket takers and cleaners in *musicking*, while Nozawa and others attend to exchanges of bodily metamessages. In the same way, *musicking* theory does not restrict music's constituent elements to sound. From this perspective, if we ask how deaf people engage with the interactive event as a whole, they can fully be participants in *musicking*.

## 4. The Relational Thinking of Music Research That Aims at “Melting Together and Becoming One”

Acoustic body theory and *musicking* theory have dismantled music's sound-centricity along different paths, but they share one feature. Both celebrate the experience of people in musical practice “melting together and becoming one.” This is “relational thinking,” which Matei Candea and others identify as a problem into which cultural anthropology has fallen. It is a normative tendency to celebrate warm connections among people without subjecting them to critique [Candea, Cook, Trundle & Yarrow, eds. 2015].

Within the framework of acoustic body theory, Yamada writes, “When one's own body is filled with resonance, and that resonance overflows the body and interpenetrates with the bodies around it, we experience vibe and groove, a transcendent and collaborative musical pleasure” [Yamada 2017: 250]. He thus values the “transcendent and collaborative musical pleasure” produced at the phenomenological level when “the other's resonance and one's own resonance melt together and become one.”

In *musicking* theory as well, smooth exchanges of feedback, such as one performer's sound being answered at once by another's, draw people into participation. Musical performance aims toward the state in which “one's own agency and the other's agency melt together and become one” as its highest goal. Behind these findings in the anthropology of music lies a normative idea that musical practice is ideal when people can participate and feel connected to one another.

It is also suggestive that the concept of “participation” in Charles Keil's groove theory, often cited by both approaches as one of their theoretical pillars, derives from Lucien Lévy-Bruhl's “participation,” or fusion [Lévy-Bruhl 1953; Keil 1987: 276]. Keil calls the minute discrepancies produced by jazz musicians' sound “participatory discrepancies” and argues that they generate a groove that draws people into performance. This groove-like “participation” resembles the principle discussed by Lévy-Bruhl through which the Bororo people of Brazil regard themselves as “araras,” or macaws. It is “participation = fusion.” Musical anthropologists do not all use the word “participation” with the meaning of “fusion” explicitly in mind, but they can be said to share this conception at its root. In research on musical performance grounded in relational thinking, participation as it ought to be goes beyond “someone joining a particular activity.” It is a process in which things that were heterogeneous fuse, or melt together and become one.

The wording “melting together and becoming one” used in this section comes from a book on sign language and dance by Yutaka Yoshida. Yoshida [2018] places signed conversation and dance practice in a Kenyan school for the deaf on the same ground by treating both as interaction. She compares the “animated bodies” that emerge in spontaneous, lively communication, such as everyday signed conversation and dance-play, with the “poised bodies” that appear in orderly interview conversations and similar settings. Focusing on the former communicative events, she describes groups of bodies that resonate and “melt together and become one” [Yoshida 2018: 171]. Yoshida's study is rich in ambitious ideas. It suggests the possibility of communication that crosses the opposition between language and nonlanguage, does not involve hearing, and can nevertheless “resonate with one another.” Yet it remains focused on dismantling a linguistic model of communication set in opposition to this possibility, without giving a logical account of what the “animated body” actually is. When deaf people mingle with others and their bodies become “animated,” for whom, and in what way, do the resonating bodies “melt together and become one”? Under what circumstances are they <em class="zh-em">unable</em> to “melt together and become one”? More fundamentally, <em class="zh-em">should</em> they “melt together and become one”?

To answer these questions, this article examines cases in which deaf people are present at sites of musical practice in a village on the Indonesian island of Bali known as the “deaf village.” Deaf people in the village do not join hearing people in playing gamelan music, dancing ritual offering dances, or listening to popular music through speakers. Yet they engage with music in their own ways. The chapters that follow examine this involvement together with its limits.[^4]

# II. Deaf Bodies That Resonate Interactively

## 1. Field Overview

Bengkala Village (Desa Bengkala), located in northern Bali, Indonesia, is often introduced by media inside and outside Indonesia as a “deaf village” or a “sign-language village.” According to village office records from January 2019, 42 of its 2,990 residents were deaf, a proportion about three to four times that of Japan and other regions. Against this background, the village developed its own sign language, Kata Kolok, hereafter “Kata Kolok Sign Language,” which is used mainly by deaf residents and their families. I conducted a total of twelve months of field research in Bengkala Village between January 2019 and March 2020. My methods included participant observation, interviews with residents, and video recording with permission.

Research has established that a specific gene, DFNB3, accounts for the high incidence of deaf births in Bengkala Village [Winata et al. 1995]. Bengkala follows the Balinese custom in which patrilineal kin groups generally live together. Because people with close genetic relations live near one another, households with deaf members tend to cluster to some extent. Thus, although deaf people make up only a little over one percent of the village population and their birth rate is declining, those whose sphere of life overlaps with deaf households feel that far more deaf people live around them than the figure of “one percent” suggests. The Kata Kolok Sign Language they use is also a familiar presence.

As members of a community in which local, familial, and linguistic communities overlap, how do Bengkala's deaf people relate to hearing residents' music in village life?

## 2. Deaf Residents and the Village Music Scene

For the residents of Bengkala Village, the most familiar forms of music, especially music as an event of performance, are the performance of bronze instrumental ensembles called gamelan, the traditional dances they accompany, and popular music.

With regard to gamelan instruments, at least within the scope of my research, I never encountered deaf residents who actively enjoyed listening to performances or joining them. Most seemed to regard the instruments as belonging to hearing people and showed little interest in them.

On the other hand, quite a few deaf people enjoy dancing. In Bengkala, however, the performing-arts domain of hearing people and that of deaf people are clearly separated. As a rule, deaf people in Bengkala Village are not permitted to dance in ritual offering performances. Hearing residents explain this by saying, “Deaf people cannot dance in precise time with the gamelan accompaniment.” Balinese dance values the close interaction between dancers and gamelan players, and the dancing body is inseparable from gamelan sound. Bengkala's deaf people, who cannot dance while listening to gamelan, are therefore regarded as unsuitable dancers for offering dances.

The village also provides separate opportunities for “deaf dance.” It is performed at welfare events, for tourists, during media coverage, or, less frequently, when requested for rites of passage. Its repertoire includes *Janger Kolok*, begun in 1967 with choreography by a hearing dancer from Gianyar Regency, which is famous for its performing arts. This piece is accompanied only by a drum and includes an audience-participation segment. The repertoire also includes three pieces accompanied by a gamelan ensemble and choreographed since 2016 by a professor at an arts university in the provincial capital, Denpasar. The dancers are deaf as a rule, while hearing people provide the gamelan accompaniment. In this way, Bengkala Village's performance settings separate hearing people as “those who can move with the music” from deaf people as “those for whom the music is adjusted.” The principal contexts of the stage are also broadly divided, with ritual for hearing people and welfare and tourism for deaf people.

This does not mean that Bengkala's deaf residents wholly disregard music in rituals. At sites of ritual offering dances, they are at least present as an audience that only watches. Interestingly, in Kata Kolok Sign Language, the word for “ritual” (*upacara*) is expressed by a motion of striking with two fingers. According to a hearing sign-language interpreter, the sign derives from the action of playing the *reyong*, a gamelan instrument consisting of a row of small, knobbed bronze gongs.[^5] The etymology of a local word cannot be taken as a direct reflection of people's conceptual world. Still, the fact that local vocabulary represents “ritual” through the action of gamelan performance, and continues to do so, suggests at least one conception shared to some degree among Bengkala's deaf residents. Gamelan music is connected with ritual and can represent ritual.

In these circumstances, which at first appear exclusionary, how do deaf residents experience the village's music scene? I will now consider in turn cases that correspond to the perspectives reviewed in the previous chapter, first acoustic-body theory and then *musicking* theory, which treats music as interaction.

## 3. Case and Interpretation from Acoustic-Body Theory 1: A Deaf Person Who Wants a Speaker

When I was lodging with a deaf family in which all seven members, from the couple to their grandchildren, were deaf, Dharma, the deaf “father,”[^6] repeatedly told me, “You should buy a speaker.” In other words, this seemed to be an indirect prompt phrased as advice to me, asking, “Could you buy a speaker for all of us?” He had previously used the same phrasing to say, “You should buy a washing machine,” so his way of prompting me was not itself surprising. What surprised me was that the object he wanted me to buy was a speaker. Apart from me, everyone in the house was deaf. A speaker should primarily be a device for hearing or listening to music. Why did he want one?

In Bali, or at least in Bengkala and the surrounding villages, almost every house has a full-sized speaker set about thirty centimeters to one meter tall. Quite a few households have no washing machine but do have a speaker. People play music at high volume while they work, or after work when they return home and relax with family and friends over tea and snacks, or sit together and pass around liquor. Most often they play Indonesian pop, K-POP, and Balinese gamelan music. In Bali, the livelier things are, the better, and people often play music loudly wherever they are. Even television volumes are extremely high.

Dharma is deaf, so he does not “hear/listen to” music from a speaker in the same way as hearing people. He said, however, that he feels loud music, especially music with heavy bass, as vibrations resonating in his heart and abdomen. In front of me, he often comically thrust his chin forward and marked the beat of Indonesian pop that someone nearby was playing loudly. Here is a body that perceives “resonance” through the skin and viscera and synchronizes, or wants to synchronize, its own movement with it. For deaf people, this tactile resonance offers one clue for “perceiving” the music that hearing people make.

Yet this experience of low-frequency vibration was not Dharma's main reason for wanting a speaker. More important to him was the connection between speakers and drinking gatherings. He enjoyed drinking and had made many hearing drinking friends outside the village. He knew that drinking gatherings seemed always to come with speakers and that hearing people seemed to enjoy themselves because of them. By getting the foreigner lodging in his home to buy a speaker, he wanted one day, when he invited friends over to drink, to make the gathering take shape more fully as a drinking gathering.

What matters here is that the host who wanted to provide the speaker was himself deaf and did not hear the sound of the music together with the other hearing people. Following Small's concept of *musicking*, musical practice can be understood as an event encompassing the actions of everyone who might be involved, even the work of ticket takers and cleaners. On this account, Dharma, a deaf person more concerned with setting the scene than with music's sounding itself, may appear to occupy a position like Small's ticket taker [cf. Small 2011]. In practice, however, his role was closer to that of a performer, or more precisely, to that of a participant in Thomas Turino's “participatory music” [Turino 2015]. Dharma wanted to provide a speaker less to set up a place where music would sound for his hearing friends than to become excited himself within the atmosphere enlivened by music. He did not want to be an outsider to music's sounding, like the ticket taker imagined by Small. He wanted to become a body resonating with others as a participant who himself sounded. To make this performer-like role of the deaf Dharma clearer, let us consider one more scene.

## 4. Case and Interpretation from Acoustic-Body Theory 2: The Sway of Music and the Sway of Intoxication

### Case 1. A Drinking Gathering Filled with Sway and Resonance

One day, January 17, 2020, at around three in the afternoon, Dharma asked me, “I'm going to visit a friend outside the village. Do you want to come?” Of course I said I would and began getting ready to leave. He loaded a fish onto his motorbike as a gift for his friend. Incidentally, this fish was the morning's “spoils.” He had taken mangoes picked without permission at his family home as a gift when visiting a fisherman's house, and the fisherman had given him the fish. Dharma's idea of visiting his friend had not come from a prior arrangement either. It came from having acquired a respectable enough gift.

After about thirty minutes by motorbike, we left the national highway for a road paved with asphalt by hand, then turned onto a winding, unpaved track of bare earth. His friend's house stood there. Although we had arrived without an appointment, the couple who owned the house showed no surprise and welcomed us with “Good to see you, good to see you,” serving tea and snacks. Both were hearing. We sat in a veranda-like space while I answered their conventional questions for a foreigner, such as where I came from. Four people from the host's family and among his brothers and friends living nearby arrived one after another. Except for the host's wife and children, all those gathered were men in their thirties or forties. The host had apparently invited them through a smartphone messaging app, saying, “Dharma is here. Let's drink.” We sat directly on the floor in a circle, and a pitcher filled to the brim with palm wine (*tuak*) and a glass were brought out. In Bali, one glass is drained in a single draught and passed around. Nearby stood a large speaker about one meter tall, connected to someone's smartphone and playing Indonesian pop at high volume.

As we passed the liquor around over dishes and snacks, everyone gradually became drunk. One man connected a microphone to the speaker and began karaoke. With his voice hoarse from liquor, the middle-aged man twisted his body and sang at full force with evident pleasure. Those around him called out, “That's good, that's good!” Dharma pointed to his friend singing karaoke, imitated his posture, and laughed, “I'll sing in a funny way.” He too was tipsy, and his face was red. Whether their bodies were swaying with the music or swaying hazily from the alcohol, everyone there, including Dharma, was making fine swaying movements.

At this moment, the deaf Dharma perceived skin-felt, visceral, and visual “vibrations” and was present as a body that was itself “vibrating.” The experience of “resonance” among the hearing people present differed greatly from Dharma's, and this difference matters. Resonances such as the melody and lyrics of the karaoke song and the singer's vocal quality could set a hearing person's body in motion, yet they did not reach Dharma. Just as the experiences of hearing people and Dharma differed, hearing people themselves must also have had various experiences of resonance depending on whether they sang by sounding their own breath, listened to someone else, called encouragement from the side, and on how intoxicated they were. In this “drinking-gathering karaoke” scene, everyone subtly swayed according to their own perceptual experience and emitted vibrations through voice or movement. The deaf Dharma was no onlooker observing the scene from outside. He was a body resonating with others, cast into the very midst of it.

In the tradition of music research, music is “humanly organized sound” [Blacking 1978], and sound will probably retain its importance in music research. Yet, as Yamada and others have shown, sound at sites of *musicking* is “resonance” that includes skin sensation, visceral sensation, heat, and atmosphere, and deaf people can also become “acoustic bodies” participating in it. When the “sound” of music becomes something that resonates through the whole body rather than something heard by the ears, sound itself need not form a barrier that prevents deaf people from participating in *musicking*.

## 5. Case and Interpretation from *Musicking* Theory 1: “Deaf Dance” under the Eaves

The previous section followed acoustic-body theory and reconceived the body participating in music as a whole-body “body resonating with others.” It thereby showed that deaf people, too, can enter the very midst of a musical setting. What makes this possible is the perception shared by deaf and hearing people of multisensory “vibration” in the broad sense. Yet the ability to perceive something is not the same as its becoming a “musical experience.” Put differently, there remains a leap between the physical phenomenon in which vibration enters one's body and resonates with other bodies and the feeling of a musical experience in which one has “melted together and become one” with others, sometimes to the point of “participation,” or fusion. From this section onward, I use *musicking* theory's perspective on music as interaction to consider the role of a deaf dancer in the unfolding interaction of a dance-play that arose suddenly under the eaves.

### Case 2. “Deaf Dance” under the Eaves

The deaf Karisma was one of the women dancers in “deaf dance.” She was forty-nine at the time of my research. She and her deaf husband had three children, two deaf and one hearing. She now lives with her hearing son's family. Recently, younger people have often performed deaf dance, but Karisma still frequently dances when the younger dancers are unavailable. Among the women on Bengkala's deaf-dance stage, she is the “veteran” who has performed most often.

For about two months during my research, I rented a room from a hearing family next door to her house. My host family and Karisma's family were relatives. They were “neighbors” and at the same time “family.”

One evening, March 17, 2019, I was resting in the courtyard after practicing a ritual offering dance with Mari, the high-school-aged daughter of my host family, and others. Her younger brother Agus was playing by beating a drum, and their mother, Ibu Mari, suggested, “Try playing the accompaniment to *Janger Kolok*.” *Janger Kolok* is one of the “deaf dances” introduced in the first section of this chapter and is usually performed in welfare and tourism settings. When Agus began to play its drum accompaniment, Ibu Mari peered into the neighboring house where the deaf Karisma was and invited her, saying, “Look, it's *Janger Kolok*. Try dancing.” Karisma slipped briskly into another room. I thought she had shyly declined, but she soon strode out while wrapping a dancing sarong around her waist. She had gone into the room to get it.

She began to dance, using the shared courtyard in front of the two houses as a stage. The hearing people who had been sitting under the eaves talking began watching while calling, “It's *Janger Kolok*!” Agus, playing the drum, kept his eyes fixed on Karisma, the dancer, and smiled. A deaf French visitor who had been staying in the village for several days happened to pass with a deaf Bengkala resident and also began watching from a short distance.

The dance reached the “audience-participation segment” in its second half. On a regular stage, a woman dancer uses her fan to select an invited guest at a welfare event, an interviewer during media coverage, or the tourist who commissioned a tourist performance. She draws that person onto the stage for an improvised dance session. Here, however, there was no “guest.” Karisma first chose Somi, Mari's friend, a young woman who enlivened any gathering. Somi immediately approached Karisma and began dancing with fierce, quick movements in a male dance form. Everyone around them collapsed with laughter. After the two danced together improvisationally for a while, Karisma prompted Somi to change dancers with the conventional greeting of palms together and a handshake. She then chose Lydia, another of Mari's friends sitting beside Somi. Laughing and saying, “No,” Lydia came out to dance as those around her urged her on. This time she made the same female dance movements as Karisma. Mari was chosen next. She tried to decline with an excuse, saying, “Look, I'm filming with the camera!” Those around her called, “Go on, go on,” and drew her onto the “stage.” After only a few seconds, Mari herself put her palms together, shook hands, and returned to her seat. Karisma laughed and signed near her hands, “Changing already.” Karisma tried to choose Ibu Mari, but Ibu Mari pointed to me to indicate that I should be next. Mari judged that Karisma had refrained from choosing me because I was filming on my smartphone. She took my phone, and Karisma chose me. When I came out to dance, everyone again collapsed with laughter, calling, “She's dancing!” After a while, I stopped and tried to prompt a change with palms together and a handshake, but Karisma pointed to tell me to turn properly as I danced. We continued dancing. When we shook hands to change, those around us pointed to the French visitor, who was then looking elsewhere, and Karisma chose him. He looked surprised, but handed his camera to the deaf resident guiding him and came out to dance. After dancing for a while and shaking hands, the two exchanged the Kata Kolok sign for “thank you,” and the French visitor returned to where he had been. Karisma performed a closing form for the participation segment with movements different from those before it. She bowed with her palms together, and the dance-play ended.

What occurred in this case was precisely what Turino [2015] calls “participatory music.” An interaction rich in relations between sound and movement and between one movement and another took shape among Agus the drummer, the dancers, the audience calling encouragement, and each person selected and drawn in to play the guest. How, then, did the excitement shared in the courtyard arise within the interaction of this dance-play?

To begin with, the occasion for this dance-play was the start of the drum part of a “deaf dance,” rather than the music of hearing people's traditional dance. Karisma went out of her way to get her dancing sarong and presented the piece before everyone as a serious dance. Those around her responded with smiles and affirmative calls. In the audience-participation segment, Karisma first chose the showy Somi, who immediately danced comically with exaggerated, highly theatrical movements. This established the direction of the segment, in which each selected person would play a guest and dance with Karisma. The suggestions of those around her and Karisma's decisions together determined whom she chose next. Once the rule of changing dancers with palms together and a handshake had been displayed and shared, either Karisma or the person playing the guest could propose a change. In this way, those watching, those watched, and those directing the flow of the performance intermingled. Focus was established [Goffman 1985], and the time-space of a participatory dance-play frame filled with laughter arose.

What deserves attention here is Karisma's striking resemblance to the skilled “caller” in American contra dance described by Turino [2015: 298]. According to Turino, callers play an important role in contra dance, a typical form of “participatory” music in which a certain number of beginners are always present. Over a microphone, they call instructions for the next movement, such as “Now, form two lines.” Callers give individual instruction on the spot to confused dancers and announce the title of the next tune, thereby directing the course of the whole event. Participatory music values drawing people in and having them participate more than the completeness of the performance. A caller must help beginners become able to dance without attending to them so much that experienced dancers grow idle. The role requires an ability to direct matters according to each situation.

Like this caller, Karisma in the present case immediately judged and chose the next dancer from the situation at the site and the people participating, thereby determining how the dance would develop. Given the context in which *Janger Kolok* is normally performed on welfare or tourism stages, the easiest people to choose first would have been me or the French tourist, both “foreigners.” I was holding up a smartphone to film, however, and the French visitor had just arrived and might not yet have grasped the situation. Karisma therefore first chose the showy Somi. Somi immediately responded with an amusing movement, and the dance-play came alive. Karisma later allowed the shy Lydia and Mari to return to the audience after a short time. When I tried to end my turn with awkward movements, she pointed to tell me to “turn properly and dance” and kept the dance going. Like a contra-dance caller, Karisma made instant judgments on each occasion from factors such as the participants' experience of Balinese dance and their willingness to take part, and she controlled the interaction's subsequent development. The atmosphere was ultimately able to continue unfolding with excitement largely because of this direction by Karisma as a veteran dancer.

## 6. Case and Interpretation from *Musicking* Theory 2: Everyday Interaction and the Interaction of Dance-Play

In fact, the hearing residents of the two households that shared this courtyard regarded Karisma as a troublemaker. Problems arising from her strained relations with the hearing son who lived with her and his wife, who was also hearing, recurred especially often. Karisma was frequently seen persistently signing to her daughter-in-law, who would casually brush her off. When her demands did not get across, Karisma would throw a tantrum and begin breaking plates and other objects. The two households, both neighbors and relatives, often struggled with this “childish” behavior.

The case concerned an impromptu dance-play in the courtyard shared by these households, exactly the same place where such problems occurred. What filled the space at this time was not the sound of plates breaking, curses flying back and forth, or faces swollen from crying. It was a group of bodies dancing, calling encouragement, and collapsing with laughter. Karisma's relations with her family, often severed through differences in language modality, became temporarily “connected” through dancing and playing together in precisely the same place. What made this possible was the structure of *Janger Kolok*, which incorporates audience “participation.” It was also the exchange of all the bodily metamessages showing concentration on the action itself, including jeers and calls, the pleasure of dancing with the body and watching dance movements, and Karisma's apt direction of dancer changes. Together, these “framing devices” [Goffman 1974] produced a “play frame” that diverged from the system of everyday order [Kajimaru 2013; Huizinga 2018; Sicart 2019]. Here, too, the deaf person was at the center of *musicking* as interaction rather than at its periphery.

Still, all the people present did not “participate” in the same way. Karisma did not perceive the “sound” of this *musicking* event surrounded by laughter and jeers, so her experience of “resonance” differed from that of the hearing people. The hearing people, too, cast their bodies into the scene through their own distinct forms of resonance. The participants already differed as dancers and audience members. Although the “audience-participation segment” unsettled this boundary, some, like Ibu Mari, steadfastly refused to come out and dance even when chosen. Others, like Mari, came out but quickly returned to the audience. The strength of the incentive to dance together differed from person to person. At this site, each person resonated with others while remaining an individual, rather than everyone uniformly “melting together and becoming one.” Two things are certain. The *musicking* event was shared there as interaction and gathered intensity in a form filled with laughter. At the center of its unfolding was a deaf dancer.

Until this point, this chapter has deliberately discussed the ways deaf people relate to Bengkala Village's music scene in relational terms. Its purpose has been to show that deaf people who do not share hearing with hearing people can participate as acoustic bodies in sites of *musicking*, especially the typical settings of “participatory music,” and can even assume a central role with the authority to determine the flow. Contrary to general “common sense,” deaf people and music are not wholly unrelated. Rather, deaf people can exist among groups of “bodies resonating with one another,” and at times even at their center.

On the other hand, Bengkala's deaf people do often experience being prevented from participating in musical practices because they are deaf. Within the bounds of “common sense,” one would probably explain, “That is because music is heard with the ears, and deaf people cannot hear.” Many hearing people in Bengkala Village do in fact explain it this way. As the preceding discussion has shown, however, that logic no longer holds. What, then, prevents deaf people from participating in musical practices and makes them solitary beings? The next chapter examines a deaf child who could not enter a dance-play at the village's integrated elementary school for deaf and hearing children. It considers what causes the “solitude” deaf people may confront in musical settings, and how they receive and deal with it. In other words, it considers a “figure of solitude in music” that much music research based on relational thinking has long overlooked.

# III. The Solitude and Sufficiency of Dance-Play

## 1. Background to the Case: A Deaf-Hearing Integrated Elementary School

In Indonesia, schools for the deaf have been established in each city as special-support schools for children with hearing impairments. In Bali, however, public transportation such as trains is virtually nonexistent. For families living far from a city, taking a child back and forth by motorcycle each day and paying for gasoline, or paying for a child to live in a dormitory, becomes a heavy burden. Many families therefore do not send their deaf children to school.

For this reason, in July 2007 one of the elementary schools in Bengkala Village began an “integrated education for deaf and hearing children” that admitted deaf children. Unlike urban schools for the deaf, which enroll only deaf children, an integrated school places several deaf children in a class composed mainly of hearing children. The school was founded on the principle that deaf and hearing children would learn together. In practice, however, a support teacher teaches a mixed-grade “class for deaf children” outside the regular classroom, and the deaf children study with hearing children only for some subjects. As a leading example of integrated education for disabled children, the school often receives inspection groups from Indonesia and abroad.

The following case took place in a fourth-grade class at this integrated elementary school. At the time, the class had eleven hearing children and two deaf children. On the day of the case, one of the deaf children was absent, so Resi was the only deaf child present.

## 2. Case: Hearing Children's Dance-Play and a Deaf Child Alone

### Case 3. Hearing Children's Dance-Play and a Deaf Child Alone

That day, April 16, 2019, the fourth-grade class was making paper pinwheels and drawing pictures. When I looked into the classroom, the teacher was gone. The room had become recess under the name of self-study and was almost a lawless space. Since it was still class time, the children seemed barely to be keeping themselves inside. The students sat around two large tables, but Resi alone had been given an individual desk and chair and sat apart from everyone else.

In this situation, a hearing child named Nopi came over and spoke to me. “Do the dance from the other day,” she said, referring to an offering dance I had performed at a ritual several days earlier. I replied, “Show me a dance.” She cheerfully began dancing, and the other children joined her. A “dance-play often seen at rituals” began. Some children remained in the center, while others, especially boys, moved in and out. They accurately danced not only the boys' and girls' offering dances that they ordinarily performed at rituals, but also “adult” offering dances such as *rejang renteng* and *janger*.

By chance, the children's dancing unfolded directly in front of Resi's desk. Resi was one of the spectators watching it. At any given time, two or three other students were also only watching, though these students continually changed. Resi did not watch without interruption. At times she returned to drawing in the notebook before her. When a boy dropped down beside her, she sometimes drove him away, saying, “Don't sit here! Go over there!”

The hearing children who danced and the spectators around them often hummed parts of the gamelan accompaniment, singing “dayon” or “nenonen.” Riding the rhythm, the dancers moved in and out, sometimes fooling around and sometimes becoming serious. They were quite literally playing through dance. The longest dance was *janger*, which includes singing. The children danced to the song and sang to the dance, producing a setting in which dance and song became one.

When the *janger* ended, the bell rang to end the class. The children learned that recess had begun and rushed outside, and the dance-play naturally disappeared. Resi followed them out. Suddenly she began dancing toward an empty corridor. What she danced did not look like Balinese dance to me. It looked like a brief pop-style movement.

Resi prompted a hearing friend with whom she often spoke to perform the movement with her. The friend glanced at me and the camera, performed Resi's movement once, then turned on her heel after only a few seconds and immediately went back to the other hearing children.

I asked Resi, “What was that dance just now?” She replied, “K-POP.”

What first draws attention in this case is Resi's “solitary” figure as she watches the “ritual dance-play” unfolding before her but does not join it. From the discussion so far, it is clear that “being unable to hear” cannot be what makes her “solitary.” Like the deaf man Dharma in Chapter II, Resi possesses a “body that resonates with others.” Like the deaf woman Karisma, she also possesses a “body that engages in *musicking*” and determines how interaction unfolds. Yet Resi did not dance with the hearing children. Was this a failure of participation? What prevented her from participating in the dance?[^7]

This article focuses on the tacit difference in domains that separates deaf people and hearing people in Bengkala Village as a powerful factor that produced Resi's “solitary figure” at this dance-play.

## 3. Interpretation 1: The Hearing Domain and the Deaf Domain

In Bengkala Village, deaf people and hearing people often carry out activities separately. One setting where this separation becomes visible is in rituals and their preparations, which occupy an important place in everyday Balinese life. Village customary law assigns the deaf group “roles” such as washing dishes, cleaning, and digging graves in preparation for rituals. Contributing to the village community through rituals is both an obligation of village membership and proof that one is, and is recognized as, a member. In Indonesia, where the social rights of disabled people are still not always taken for granted, hearing residents often describe this arrangement proudly to outside inspection groups. At the same time, assigning a particular role to the deaf group also confines the group within that role. As a result, deaf people and hearing people work at different times and in different places during ritual preparations. Their opportunities to interact through this work are extremely limited.

Differences between the times and places of deaf and hearing people's activities also arise where no external force such as customary law is at work. For example, when I joined the work of the village youth association with Mari, the hearing girl who appeared in Case 2, I suddenly noticed that no deaf people were present. I wondered why and asked her. Looking at a loss for an answer, she gave an awkward smile and said, “It's not that they're prohibited from joining, but you know, they're [different from us], aren't they?” (fieldnotes, March 6, 2019). The hearing residents' sense that “they, the deaf people, are different from us” was seldom actively put into words, but seemed to be shared among hearing residents to some degree. Deaf residents, meanwhile, shared a sense that “they, the hearing people, are different from us.” How, then, was this “tacit difference in domains” formed, and what kind of difference was it?

To begin with, residents did not necessarily categorize the tacit “deaf domain” only as negative “discrimination.” Deaf people in Bengkala, for example, often said, “It is good to be deaf.” Their reason was not the one they often gave, or were made to give, in media interviews, that “in this village, deaf people are treated the same as hearing people.” Known as a “deaf village,” Bengkala receives visits from domestic and international media, inspection groups, and tourists, as well as corporate social-responsibility welfare assistance from a large oil company. On these occasions, deaf residents become the “main figures.” In other words, deaf residents also enjoy outside attention and donations as benefits of their own domain. Once, when a Jakarta company held an event for deaf people in the village, a deaf person who had missed out on the event gifts became angry with a hearing person who had received one. This can be understood as a protest against a hearing person's encroachment on the deaf domain.

Although Bengkala's deaf residents live together with hearing people every day, customary law, the everyday community, and the gaze of outsiders distinguish them as people different from hearing people. This distinction produces a tacit difference in domains between deaf and hearing people in daily life. The greatest factor in this difference, however, is not the hearing impairment that defines the category of “deaf people” in the first place. It is the difference in linguistic modality between spoken language and sign language, together with the accumulated experiences of failed communication that follow from it.

On one occasion, January 10, 2019, I was speaking in Kata Kolok Sign Language with a deaf child at the village elementary school. In the middle of our conversation, a hearing teacher standing beside us took hold of my arm and spoke to me aloud. I let the teacher interrupt me, turned around, and replied in spoken language. The deaf child whose conversation had been interrupted did not appear angry. She simply watched our faces as we continued speaking in sounds she could not hear.

Events like this occur frequently around deaf people. The moment a conversational partner begins using spoken language, a deaf person is cut off from the content of the conversation. It is far easier to break into a signed conversation with spoken language than to break into a spoken conversation with sign language, which requires first gaining the other person's attention. This was the violence of spoken language against a deaf person that both the hearing teacher who broke into my signed conversation and I, who responded, enacted without noticing. The deaf child did not become angry when spoken language interrupted her conversation because this was her everyday life. If she became angry every time, there would be no end to it.

Mara Green, who studies village sign-language communication in Nepal, argues that for signers, “understanding” refers not simply to knowledge of grammar and vocabulary. It is a question of attitude and ethics, of whether people are willing to use sign language with one another and try to understand one another [Green 2014]. The same can be seen in Bengkala. Deaf people there often judge others by saying, “That person can sign” or “That person cannot sign.” Dharma, for example, spoke with certainty of a hearing friend from outside the village whom he had met only twice. “That guy can sign,” he said. “Well, he's still not very good, but I'll teach him from now on.” By contrast, the people he judged unable to sign were hearing residents close to him in the village. They included hearing residents with deaf family members, whom the international deaf community calls CODAs, or Children of Deaf Adults, and SODAs, or Siblings of Deaf Adults or Children. These hearing people had grown up with deaf people. They had always seen their deaf family members using Kata Kolok Sign Language and had always faced the need to communicate, so they should have had more sign-language ability than a hearing person from a remote mountain area who had met deaf people only twice. Yet Dharma said that they did not understand our sign language and did not speak to us in signs that meant anything to us. No matter how many signs one knows as knowledge, if one does not actually use them, it is the same as not knowing them. Conversely, as long as one tries to communicate despite lacking knowledge, the two parties will come to “understand” something, although the process and result will doubtless include many misunderstandings. As Green suggests, for deaf people in Bengkala, understanding in communication means trying to understand and trying to understand one another.

These experiences of communication among deaf people in Bengkala show that the village's “difference in domains” between hearing and deaf people is produced most powerfully neither by the perceptual categories of “hearing” and “deaf,” nor by the disability-based categories of “nondisabled” and “disabled.” It is produced by the linguistic difference between “spoken language” and “sign language,” and by the question of how people try to communicate. Deaf people do not try to join offering-dance practice because they cannot dance to the accompaniment. They stay away because only hearing people are there, and those people either do not know Kata Kolok Sign Language or, even if they know it, do not try to speak actively in it. This tacit difference in domains, underpinned by accumulated experiences of failed communication, clearly separates “deaf people's dance” from “hearing people's dance” even in performance settings. To repeat, deaf people possess both “bodies that resonate with one another” and “bodies that engage in *musicking*.”

Let us now return to the classroom case. Like adults in the village, most students could use Kata Kolok Sign Language only well enough to exchange greetings. The deaf children also studied in the “class for deaf children” outside the regular classroom, so deaf and hearing children often spent their time separately. The gaze of outside inspection groups continually divided the students into deaf and hearing children and reinforced the boundary. As a result, even at the elementary school, hearing children tended to gather and play with hearing children, while deaf children gathered with deaf children. For Resi, actively entering a dance-play consisting only of hearing children was probably very difficult in terms of her personal relations.

Seeing a deaf child who alone does not join the hearing children's dance-play, many people would think, “It is because deaf people cannot dance with the music.” In fact, this is the logic used in Bengkala to explain why deaf people do not become dancers in ritual offering dances. Yet, as Chapter II argued, whether deaf people hear sound and whether they participate in music are entirely different questions. The barrier that made Resi “solitary” in this classroom was not the sonic character of music. It was the social, tacit difference in domains entangled with this context, and the accumulated experiences of failed communication around sign language that lay at its base.

## 4. Interpretation 2: K-POP That Fulfills

Resi did not join the hearing children's dance-play, but immediately after it dispersed she danced K-POP outside the classroom. She did not dance the Balinese traditional dances she had seen countless times. Why did she dance K-POP alone? How might her figure “alone” be interpreted?

At the time of my research, the deaf girls in Bengkala were devoted to K-POP idols. Resi was one of them. Each day she looked up information about groups online, watched music videos, imitated their dancing, and used K-POP images for her Instagram profile. For her, K-POP was not something heard with the ears but a glittering, cool, and sometimes sexy image of stars and dance experienced visually and through video. At the same time, it was evoked together with the bodily memory of imitating those dances.

On one occasion, March 12, 2019, a sign-language support teacher at the elementary school invited me to visit Resi's house with her. Resi, dressed in ordinary clothes, and her mother welcomed us. Her mother said that no one in the house except Resi could use Kata Kolok Sign Language. Resi herself also said that no one in her family could sign. I asked her mother, “How do you usually communicate?” She replied, “We use something like sign language inside the house.” These were home signs, signs used within a family that includes a deaf person. I told her mother about Resi at school. “She's lively and energetic. I hear she's interested in Japan and Korea and wants to work in one of those countries someday.” Her mother gave a strained smile. “She's nothing like that at home. She always shuts herself in her room alone and does nothing but look at her phone.” Later, I asked Resi, “What do you usually do at home?” She said that she looked up K-POP idols or browsed Instagram. From her parents' perspective, their daughter, with whom they could not communicate as they did through spoken language, shut herself in her room and did nothing but use her phone. For Resi herself, it was a rich world connected to the outside.

The deaf children living in rural Bali had, of course, never attended an idol's live performance. Being deaf, they had also never heard the songs as sound. For them, the musical genre of K-POP existed on screens as highly edited, cool still and moving images, together with the stories that accompanied them. Yet they were certainly <em class="zh-em">connected</em> to this thoroughly visual and video-based K-POP.

At the time, K-POP was enormously popular among hearing children as well as deaf children. In fact, immediately after dancing alone, Resi invited a hearing friend to join her. Her act of dancing K-POP may therefore also have had the strategic aspect of a “desirable frame” attentive to the hearing children around her, as well as to me holding a camera. Even so, this article seeks to interpret her “figure dancing alone” from a perspective distinct both from the physical and phenomenological resonance of acoustic-body theory and from the socially interactive relations with others described by *musicking* theory. If we adopt a relational position that takes connection with others as music's highest goal and analyze Resi only through her relations with others, she appears to be a “failed case” that did not “melt together and become one.” She did not join the dance-play inside the classroom, and her friend rejected her K-POP dance outside it after only a few seconds. But was she really a “failed case”?

When Resi danced K-POP alone outside the classroom in this case, her gaze rested on the void (*kū*) as though she had entered another world. As described above, Balinese traditional dance in Bengkala is clearly divided between hearing people's domain of ritual offering performance and deaf people's domain of welfare-related contexts. Yet stages belonging to the latter domain, as well as participation in offering-ritual practice, are open to deaf people, so their access to dance is not entirely restricted. While the dance-play in this case unfolded, Resi repeatedly watched the hearing children at play in dance, sometimes smiled, and even made movements as though imitating them. She was not wholly uninterested in Balinese traditional dance. Resi herself said, however, that she did not intend to dance it. For her, K-POP was the closest, coolest form, and the one in which she could most fully “immerse herself.” Outside the classroom, she danced a phrase of K-POP rather than the Balinese traditional dances she had seen so many times or some wholly different game. Did she do so because she was oriented toward immersing herself in that dance? This article calls this mode, in which a person is oriented toward and filled with the experience of the activity she herself is carrying out at that very moment, “self-sufficiency.”

It is important that the object toward which the “self” is oriented here is not “the person herself,” but “the experience of the activity the person herself is carrying out at this very moment.” In this article, a state of “self-sufficiency” does not necessarily mean separation from others. Because it includes the possibility that an experience may become sufficient precisely through activity with others, it remains open to others while placing the focus on the person's own experience. Resi was not dancing alone in a room. She danced outside the classroom amid the gazes of her friends and of me, in the very midst of social relations. This does not conflict with the possibility that she experienced self-sufficiency through K-POP.

When she danced that one phrase, the accumulation of K-POP images she had once seen on a screen propelled her small body. What she experienced in the school corridor was a self-sufficient K-POP world. While she remained situated within the actual social relation of “me and my friends,” the fictive new relation of “me and K-POP images” emerged. This “new relation” resembles the experience of Mongolian Christian converts discussed by Morten Pedersen, one of the leading figures of post-relationalism. They separated themselves from the actual social relation of “me and my relatives” and formed a new relation between “me and God” [Pedersen 2012]. At the same time, what matters here is that Resi's new relation arose precisely through dance and was fragile enough to vanish in only a few seconds.

Thomas Turino, using James Lea's terms the Possible and the Actual, argues that everyday life takes place in an Actual dominated by familiar knowledge and habits grounded in past experience. Art awakens us from habit. Within a special frame that permits immersion in the “here and now,” it dramatically foregrounds the interaction of the Actual with future possibilities, namely dreams, hopes, desires, and ideals, or the Possible. In doing so, art crystallizes the essence of a fulfilled life [Lea 2001, via Turino 2015: 41-43]. Music, visual art, theater, and other arts are used in rituals in many parts of the world. In light of this fact, Turino's location of the “power of art” in a balanced interaction between the Actual and the Possible is reasonable. Looking again at Resi, it was precisely the rhythmic movement of K-POP, rather than simply walking or imagining it in her mind, that could make the Possible strongly present in a “here and now” connected to the Actual. That Possible consisted of the glittering images of K-POP idols she had seen before, or an “impossible” future image such as sharing a stage with them. Yet the power of dance to bring this into the foreground was less stable than Turino believed. Resi's K-POP was rejected by her friend and disappeared after only a few dozen seconds.

As long as we live in this world, complete escape from the mesh of relations remains impossible. In this sense, post-relationalism does not posit a world without relations. Yet when we take one step beyond relational thinking, which has focused on social relations and assigned them positive meaning, and look at a deaf child dancing K-POP alone, we see a figure oriented through dance and music toward what she herself is experiencing at that very moment and becoming self-sufficient. We also see the richness and fragility of that experience. In “Cutting the Network,” Marilyn Strathern argues that when elements become a sum, the network is cut and brought to a stop [Strathern 1996: 523]. Sufficiency is precisely one form of such a sum. It can cut an existing relation and provide the occasion for a wholly different kind of new relation to begin. When a deaf girl dances K-POP alone, her figure need not express only “pitiful loneliness.” It also reveals what might be called “dignified solitude,” filled with self-sufficiency as a new relationality.

# IV. Conclusion: Everyone Is Self-Sufficient

This article began from the common-sense idea that “deaf people who cannot hear cannot participate in music, an art of the ears.” Drawing on perspectives in the anthropology of music that have dismantled and expanded the sound-centeredness of music, especially acoustic-body theory and *musicking* theory, it discussed how deaf people in Bengkala Village do and do not “participate” in hearing people's musical practices. Chapter II described two deaf bodies that participate in music. The first was a “body that resonates with others,” the body of a deaf person who nonetheless wants a speaker and sways in a karaoke setting while intoxicated and feeling bass vibrations in the viscera. The second was a “body that engages in *musicking*,” the body of a deaf person who determines the development of interaction and greatly heightens a performance during a dance-play that suddenly arises under the eaves. In both cases, the body “participates” in musical performance through “resonance” that includes visceral sensation and atmosphere, riding rhythm and engaging in smooth exchanges with others. Chapter III, by contrast, examined a deaf child who appeared “solitary” at first glance. She did not join her friends' dance-play as a dancer, engaged in no explicit exchange, and afterward danced alone. The article showed that the barrier to her participation in music was the tacit difference in domains between deaf and hearing people created by accumulated experiences of failed communication around sign language, rather than a difference in hearing. At the same time, it showed that the time-space in which she danced alone contained a self-sufficient moment oriented toward the experience of her own activity. In this concluding chapter, I extend the search for conditions of musical participation and non-participation from deaf people to hearing people as well, and consider from a post-relational position what it means for a human being to participate in music.

Small's *Musicking*, which set out a paradigm for discussing music through the relations intertwined in musical practice, closes with a final chapter titled “The Solitary Flute Player” [Small 2011]. As a thought experiment, and with a romanticist gaze [Mori 2017], Small imagines an African herdsman playing a flute alone on the grassland and poses a question to the reader. The herdsman certainly seems to be a “solitary” being cut off from the rich relationality of *musicking*, but is he really? Small then shows that even this herdsman, however “solitary” he appears outwardly, remains connected to society and the world because the instrument's design, tuning, melodic patterns, and other elements are made by society [Small 2011: 379-380]. Small thus ends up returning even the “solitary flute player” to the mesh of relations in *musicking*. Could there also be an approach that finds in him a self-sufficient moment like the one found in Chapter III? The herdsman probably played that tune on his flute at that moment because he felt like playing it. He might remain immersed in it for hours, or want to stop after only a few seconds. Even in the time-space of playing a flute alone on the grassland, a person cannot escape the mesh of social relations. Yet a moment of sufficiency does exist there, a moment in which the player immerses himself in the activity of playing the flute.

Looking in the other direction, the people discussed in Chapter II who participate in musical performances with others can also be understood as each becoming self-sufficient to varying degrees. In karaoke at a drinking gathering, the perception and cognition of every actor must differ not only between deaf and hearing people, but among hearing people as well. In his ethnography of noise music in Japan, David Novak writes, “This ecstasy at high volume arises not from social collectivity but from a personal encounter with the overwhelming presence of sound” [Novak 2019: 65]. In noise music, known for its high-volume performances in underground live venues, participants simply immerse themselves in a “personal encounter with the overwhelming presence of sound” entering their own bodies. Irresistible resonance, especially as it grows louder, can bring those present a self-sufficiency in which they can think only of themselves and the activity taking place. Before people resonate with others, they have already experienced a “personal encounter” with resonance entering their own bodies.

In the case of the dance-play under the eaves, too, the incentives to become a dancer varied, and the development of interaction was filled with uncertain elements. Precisely because events did not necessarily proceed according to an individual's wishes, they contained an element of challenge. The focused and immersive “flow experience” described by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, which is one form of self-sufficiency, could arise through the subtleties of exchange [Csikszentmihalyi 2000; Turino 2015]. Rather than everyone becoming sufficient in the same way, it is the presence of different forms of self-sufficiency that makes the unfolding of interaction “interesting.” From this perspective, even in musical performances that proponents of relational thinking call “successful,” people may only appear to “melt together and become one.” Might each person in fact be moving back and forth among fragile parameters of self-sufficiency?

Music is not made of sound alone. It is an interactive event of whole-body mutual resonance. Within it, “participants” are oriented toward the very thing they are experiencing at that moment and experience moments of self-sufficiency. Whether alone or among others, these moments arise in varying degrees and forms. In the time-space of music, as “bodies that resonate with one another and engage in *musicking*,” everyone is solitary, and everyone also plays in the intervals of sufficiency, in all their variations. Here there is no difference between hearing people and deaf people.
