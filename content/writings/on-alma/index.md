> There are good reasons to accept that the Christian topic of immaculate conception is grounded in the mistranslation of the Hebrew alma (which simply means ‘young woman’) as ‘virgin’: ‘It would appear that Western civilization has endured two millennia of consecrated sexual neurosis simply because the authors of Matthew and Luke could not read Hebrew.’ There are also good reasons to accept that the seventy ‘virgins’ awaiting martyrs in the Muslim paradise resulted from a mistranslation: in using the word hur, transliterated as ‘houris,’ Koran relied here on the early Christian texts which used the Aramaic hur, meaning ‘white raisins,’ a delicacy. Let us take a young martyr on a suicide mission because he took literally his leader’s promise: ‘The gates of Paradise have opened for you. There are beautiful black-eyed virgins waiting for you on the banks of rivers of honey.’ Imagine the look on his face ‘when, finding himself in a paradise teeming with his fellow thugs, his seventy houris arrive as a fistful of raisins.’[^1]

<span class="lead">Ž</span>ižek opens this seemingly electrifying controversy with a notoriously compressed verdict, one that all but re-paraphrases a point made in Sam Harris’s *The End of Faith*.[^2] Before we come to the etymology, though, the passage carries two errors that ought to be cleared away first.

The first is a matter of terminology. Žižek, following Harris, uses *Immaculate Conception* for Mary’s conceiving Jesus without human agency. In Catholic teaching, however, the Immaculate Conception says otherwise: that Mary, from the first moment of her own conception in her mother’s womb, was preserved from original sin, a doctrine Pius IX defined as binding dogma in *Ineffabilis Deus* (1854). The doctrine that Mary conceived Jesus by the Holy Spirit and without intercourse goes by its own name, the *virginal conception*.[^3] The note in the Chinese edition of this book,

> **Immaculate Conception** 天主教認為，聖母瑪麗亞並非交媾所生，而是其母聖安妮感上帝之靈而孕生，因此也避免了原罪，是純潔無瑕的。[^21]
> 
> ***Immaculate Conception** According to Catholic doctrine, the Virgin Mary was not conceived through sexual intercourse, but was conceived by her mother, Saint Anne, through the inspiration of the Spirit of God; therefore she was also spared original sin and is pure and immaculate.*

[^21]: See Footnote 1.

pushes the confusion a step further and states flatly that Catholicism holds “the Virgin Mary was not begotten by intercourse; her mother Saint Anne conceived her upon being touched by the Spirit of God,” which transplants the template of the virgin birth one generation up, onto Anne. The doctrinal tradition never claimed that Anne conceived asexually. Joachim and Anne begot Mary in the entirely usual way and the dogma concerns only whether that embryo contracted original sin.

The second is the target of the charge. In the quip “the authors of Matthew and Luke could not read Hebrew,” Luke is wrongly accused: the Gospel of Luke never quotes Isaiah 7:14 at all. Only Matthew cites the verse and declares it fulfilled (Matt 1:22–23). Luke does call Mary παρθένος twice in the Annunciation narrative (Luke 1:27), but he never appeals to Isaiah. Even if the charge of mistranslation stood, then, it would touch at most Matthew’s fulfilment citation, not the source of the virgin-birth narrative itself.

***
The genuinely early form of this argument goes back to Justin Martyr’s *Dialogue with Trypho*, from around 155–160 CE. In chapter 67 Justin has his Jewish interlocutor Trypho state the objection in his own words: the text should read not “Behold, the virgin shall conceive” but “Behold, the young woman shall conceive.” In chapter 71 Justin goes on the offensive and accuses the Jewish teachers of tampering with Scripture and translating it falsely; in chapter 84 he makes the “sign” the core of his apologetic case: an ordinary young woman conceiving and giving birth in the ordinary way would hardly amount to the miraculous sign Isaiah announces.[^4] Around 180 CE, Irenaeus supplies the second important link in *Against Heresies* 3.21. He says plainly that some interpreters take Isaiah 7:14 as “Behold, a young woman shall conceive,” and he ties that reading to Theodotion of Ephesus and Aquila of Pontus, both of whom he calls Jewish proselytes. His answer is to insist on the authority and the chronological priority of the Septuagint, which predates Christianity, therefore <ruby lang="grc">παρθένος<rt>parthénos</rt></ruby> cannot be explained away as something Christians invented after the fact to serve the nativity story.[^5] By the middle of the third century (Celsus wrote around 177; Origen answered around 248), Origen in *Contra Celsum* 1.34–35 imagines the Jewish opponent “splitting words” and objecting that the text says not “Lo, a virgin” but “Lo, a young woman”; he too returns to the “sign,” arguing that an ordinary young woman bearing a son in the ordinary way could hardly carry its weight in Isaiah’s context.[^6]

One thing is worth adding here: the “other translations” Irenaeus names are real philological entities. At Isaiah 7:14 the Greek versions of Aquila, Theodotion, and Symmachus all read ἡ <ruby lang="grc">νεᾶνις<rt>neânis</rt></ruby>, “young woman,” the neutral and literal equivalent of the Hebrew.[^7] The quarrel over παρθένος and νεᾶνις, in other words, has been running since the second century, with professional translations to cite on either side; to take it for a fresh discovery of modern sceptics is the most common illusion in this reception history.

<ruby lang="he">עַלְמָה<rt>ʿalmāh</rt></ruby> means a young woman of marriageable age. Given the plausible assumptions of ancient Semitic society, such a woman was ordinarily unmarried, and ordinarily a virgin.[^8] The Septuagint’s own habits confirm this. Elsewhere in the Bible עַלְמָה usually becomes νεᾶνις (in Exodus 2:8, the Miriam who watches over the infant Moses is ἡ νεᾶνις), and παρθένος appears only at Genesis 24:43, for Rebekah, and at Isaiah 7:14; the Rebekah passage, moreover, has the backing of Genesis 24:16, which states her virginity outright with <ruby lang="he">בְּתוּלָה<rt>bəṯûlāh</rt></ruby>. The παρθένος of Isaiah 7:14 is therefore a marked choice within the translators’ own practice, and whether it reflects theological foresight, or simply how broad παρθένος then was, is exactly where the whole quarrel begins.

The critics’ standard line runs: “If Isaiah had meant a virgin, he would have written <ruby lang="he">בְּתוּלָה<rt>bəṯûlāh</rt></ruby>.” The first problem is that this word is no strict technical term either. Joel 1:8 has a בְּתוּלָה girding sackcloth to wail for <ruby lang="he">בַּעַל נְעוּרֶיהָ<rt>baʿal nəʿûreyhā</rt></ruby>, “the husband of her youth.” The ordinary sense of <ruby lang="he">בַּעַל<rt>baʿal</rt></ruby> is husband, not fiancé; most translators, finding a virgin in mourning for her husband too incongruous, put “betrothed” instead, and that decision itself betrays that בְּתוּלָה, outside legal contexts, is first of all a word of age and status. Wenham’s classic study argues on this basis that its core sense is “a girl of marriageable age.”[^9]

***

Almost, because of two textual details. First, the Septuagint manuscripts split at the verb: one branch has <ruby lang="grc">ἕξει<rt>héxei</rt></ruby> (“shall have”), the other <ruby lang="grc">λήμψεται<rt>lḗmpsetai</rt></ruby> (“shall receive,” “shall conceive”). Rahlfs prints the former; Swete, following Codex Vaticanus, prints the latter; the standard text of Matthew (NA28) has ἕξει. Whether Matthew reworked a λήμψεται-type Vorlage into ἕξει, or simply had a ἕξει-type Septuagint at hand, remains an open question.[^10] Second, the uncontested Matthean touch: Isaiah’s second-person singular <ruby lang="grc">καλέσεις<rt>kaléseis</rt></ruby> (“you shall call his name”) becomes, in Matthew’s citation, the third-person plural <ruby lang="grc">καλέσουσιν<rt>kalésousin</rt></ruby> (“they shall call his name”).

παρθένος should be treated first as a word of social and age category: “maiden,” an unmarried girl, in many contexts indeed a virgin. But it has no necessary lexical bond to virginity. LSJ marks a use “of unmarried women who are not virgins,” citing precisely Iliad 2.514, where Astyoche, a <ruby lang="grc">παρθένος αἰδοίη<rt>parthénos aidoíē</rt></ruby> (“respected parthenos”), bears the war-god Ares two sons, Ascalaphus and Ialmenus.[^11] The same holds for the etymology. The origin of παρθένος is not fully secure; the currently influential proposal is Klingenschmitt’s \*pr̥-sténos (“with protruding breasts”), which Beekes accepts in his etymological dictionary. If it holds, the word’s older semantic centre is not abstract moral purity but the pubescent, marriageable girl. Janse notes that in Hellenistic and imperial documentary evidence, too, παρθένος tends first of all to mark a girl’s age class. The reconstruction has met resistance on both phonological and semantic grounds, however, and should not be taken as settled.[^12]

In the context of Isaiah 7:14 and Matthew 1:23, then, παρθένος says more than <ruby lang="he">עַלְמָה<rt>ʿalmāh</rt></ruby>, yet still less than the modern physiological or legal “virgin.” It carries the social presumption of unmarried maidenhood, and narrative and theology can narrow that presumption into virginity in the sexual sense. Matthew 1 supplies exactly that setting: that Mary had not yet been taken home (1:18) and that Joseph had not known her (1:25) are both made explicit themes, and the narrative locks παρθένος into “virgin.”

de Vaan glosses *virgo* as “girl of marriageable age; virgin,” and the derivatives *virginalis*, *virginarius*, *virgineus* hover between “marriageable girl” and “maiden” in the same way. He records the older Walde–Hofmann connection of *virgo* with *virga* (“shoot, twig”) and rejects the analysis of the word as a compound of “man” and “woman.”[^13] In other words, *virgo* is itself a Latin word in which marriageable status and bodily virginity sit close together: not a neutral counterpart of Hebrew <ruby lang="he">עַלְמָה<rt>ʿalmāh</rt></ruby>, but a ready-made vehicle whose ordinary semantic field happens to accommodate the doctrinal sense “virgin.”

Return now to Žižek’s charge of “not being able to read Hebrew,” and its fragility shows at once. Jerome is the very owner of *hebraica veritas* (“the Hebrew truth,” the programmatic slogan of his retranslation of the Old Testament from the Hebrew): the whole point of his project was to get behind the Septuagint to the Hebrew original. He evidently knew that the more neutral options *puella* and *adulescentula* were available, and elsewhere he used them for the same root; here, nonetheless, he wrote *ecce virgo concipiet*. In *Adversus Jovinianum* he takes on the Jewish argument that *ALMA* is no virgin and that the proper word for one would be *BETHULA*, and he explains *ALMA* as *abscondita virgo*, “a hidden-away virgin.”[^14] All of this looks like an exegetical and theological choice made in full knowledge. The verse had, after all, been publicly fought over for two centuries by his time, ever since Justin; had Jerome’s *virgo* sprung from ignorance, one can easily imagine his contemporaries tearing him apart, since taking it for incompetence requires far more imagination than taking it for tendentiousness.

In Harris’s account this tightening naturally figures as a drift born of ignorance. But we now know that the Christian doctrine and narrative of the virgin birth never hung on the single word of Isaiah 7:14; where the tightening happened, it was only a fulfilment answering to the grand biblical context.

***
Even if we stretch a word’s possible range of meaning in its social setting to the neutral maximum, that range alone is not a sufficient condition for deriving doctrine, or for demolishing it; religious traditions have internal mechanisms of their own for correcting and fixing interpretation. Take the legend of Saint Simeon the God-receiver preserved by the Orthodox Church. The old man of Luke 2 who receives the infant Jesus in the Temple is identified in hagiography as one of the Seventy translators: while translating Isaiah 7:14 he suspected that παρθένος could not be right and wished to correct it to “woman,” whereupon an angel stayed his hand and promised that he would not taste death before he saw the words fulfilled.[^15] Legends of this kind are of course no philological evidence (Oancea’s search through the layered sources stops at a Byzantine archetype). What they attest is a kind of tradition in which the final warrant of a reading is never lexicographical but epiphanical. Semantic argument and doctrinal argument run on different planes. (This is also why “a single mistranslated word underwrote two millennia of consecrated sexual neurosis,” taken as philology or as history of religion alike, though hilarious in its cunty-counter-apologetical way, is unfortunately infelicitous.)

***
The case of Qurʾānic <ruby lang="ar">حُور<rt>ḥūr</rt></ruby> differs from Isaiah 7:14 in its very structure. To begin with, the European “houri” is a back-formation that came through Persian and Turkish; the singular form \*ḥūrī never occurs in the Qurʾān. What the Qurʾān has is the plural <ruby lang="ar">حُور<rt>ḥūr</rt></ruby>, four times in all (44:54, 52:20, 55:72, 56:22), three of them joined with <ruby lang="ar">عِين<rt>ʿīn</rt></ruby>. Grammatically ḥūr is the plural of an adjective, masculine ʾaḥwar, feminine ḥawrāʾ. The classical lexica explain the root sense, ḥawar, as the intense whiteness of the white of the eye against the intense blackness of the iris, then whiteness or fairness in general, and even purity in the moral sense; ʿīn is the plural of ʾaʿyan / ʿaynāʾ, “large-eyed, with well-marked eyes.”[^16] Take 44:54 as the example:

Literally this says no more than “We paired them with the white-and-bright-eyed.” Within Qurʾānic Arabic the phrase is hardly a mystery: it describes companions of the Garden marked by beauty of eye and fairness of complexion. That <ruby lang="ar">حُور<rt>ḥūr</rt></ruby> later hardened into servile female companions is the famous joint product of the Qurʾānic text, the exegetical tradition, the hadith material, popular imagination, and European Orientalist reception. In particular, the figure of “seventy-two virgins” (not the seventy Žižek gives) comes from the hadith literature, such as the six rewards of the martyr recorded by al-Tirmidhī,[^17] and is not an expression of the Qurʾānic text itself.

The claim that <ruby lang="ar">حُور<rt>ḥūr</rt></ruby> originally meant “white grapes” or “raisins” belongs to Christoph Luxenberg’s brand of Syro-Aramaic re-reading, which leans chiefly on the grape imagery in the *Hymns on Paradise* of the fourth-century Syriac father Ephrem. Scholars have mostly answered with sharp criticism: de Blois’s review catalogues technical errors on both the Syriac and the Arabic side and calls the author a dilettante outright, and even those sympathetic to the “Syriac background” approach, such as El-Badawi, are clear that ḥūr ʿīn refers to female companions and not to grapes. What remains genuinely open to discussion is only whether these paradisal wedding scenes draw on the bridal-chamber imagery of Syriac church literature.[^18] The case is not parallel to Isaiah 7:14, which has a visible chain of philological history. The “raisin” account of ḥūr requires a change of interpretive frame: out of the Arabic text of the Qurʾān and into a hypothesised Syriac substrate or etymological system.[^19]

Still, since the root ḥ-w-r straddles “white” and “pure” in Arabic and Syriac alike, a far milder reception-history hypothesis is available: the whiteness of the maidens of the Garden may originally have carried the sense of spiritual purity, which commentary and popular imagination later literalised into complexion and sensory detail. Early Christian imagination already housed entirely non-erotic maidens of heaven: the twelve white-clad virgins guarding the tower in the ninth Similitude of the *Shepherd of Hermas* are allegorical embodiments of holy spirits, the powers of the Son of God: Faith, Continence, Purity, Truth, and the rest.[^20] The image of the heavenly maiden in Near Eastern religious imagination is not necessarily erotic; the eroticisation belongs to a particular reception history.

***
In Chinese, one further layer of error appears, and it is worth spelling out because it completes the pattern. The English *Immaculate Conception*, if intuited as “a conception without stain,” is easily read as “a conception without sex”; the loose Chinese phrase <ruby>聖母感孕<rt>shèngmǔ gǎnyùn</rt></ruby>, roughly “the Holy Mother conceived upon being touched by the divine,” reinforces exactly that misreading, since it sounds like a statement about how Mary became pregnant. The more accurate Chinese term <ruby>聖母無染原罪<rt>shèngmǔ wú rǎn yuánzuì</rt></ruby>, “the Holy Mother unstained by original sin,” puts the weight back on sin and grace, and the fullest form <ruby>聖母始胎無染原罪<rt>shèngmǔ shǐtāi wú rǎn yuánzuì</rt></ruby>, “unstained by original sin from the very conception,” preserves the time-point of *conception* as well. Five distinct shifts, then, have taken place here: the sense of the Hebrew word narrowed in the Greek version; the Greek version was Christologised in Matthew; the Latin and European vernacular traditions fixed that sense; modern English writers use *Immaculate Conception* where they mean the *virginal conception*; and modern Chinese annotation stirs the two together again into 聖母感孕. For Žižek’s joke to work, only the last two shifts are needed, and those two have nothing at all to do with Hebrew.
