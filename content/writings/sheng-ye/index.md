<p class="no-indent">Zhou Zuoren, from <em>Talks on Tigers</em>.</p>

<span class="lead">C</span>hancing to look into the *Sutra of the Bodhisattva Precepts*, I found it there said that anyone who has received the Bodhisattva precepts, if he sees what living creatures are doing and will not join them in it, or will not look in upon a sick man, or will not comfort the sorrowful and vexed, in every such case incurs a defiled transgression; there being but a few exceptional cases in which there is no offence, one being that he is himself cultivating a higher work and does not wish to leave it off even for a while. This touched me deeply, and I made up my mind to be off and cultivate my own higher work.

Perhaps someone will ask, “You? You too have a higher work?” Yes. Each man has a higher work of his own; though the works differ one from another, their being higher works is one and the same. The proverb has it, “A toad to prop a bed-leg.” Now, though a toad is ugly, at least its venom may be taken from it; put it beneath a bed-leg, and the strength of the toad falls short even of a piece of broken tile. I am neither a born satirist nor a prophetic moralist; I can neither produce ten volumes of *The Analects* for children to learn by heart, nor compile *The Extensive Record of the Forest of Laughter* for refined and vulgar alike to enjoy. What, then, is all my lofty talk and large discourse for? A hedge-monk mounting the high seat to discourse at random upon *Prajñā* would do better to remain in his cell and render a few chapters of Dharma-verses; it would be of more use. My higher work, therefore, lies in ceasing to manufacture (words of lofty talk and large discourse) and actually plying the pedlar’s trade. Other men’s thoughts are always loftier than mine; other men’s writing is always more exquisite than mine: if I desert the dark and go over to the light, would that not be the highest of higher works? Yet this is higher only in my case. As for other men, each naturally has his own excellence: an expedition against Mongolia, perhaps, or buying a concubine, or honouring Confucius, or taking snuff—none is inadmissible; for the man whom it suits, each is his higher work.

*(Eighth month of the tenth year [August 1921], at the Western Hills.)*
