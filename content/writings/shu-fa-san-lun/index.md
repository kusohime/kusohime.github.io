> **Text and textual basis**: Fu Shan, “Miscellaneous Remarks on Calligraphy.” This translation follows the sequence of nineteen remarks in the [Chinese Wikisource transcription](https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/%E6%9B%B8%E6%B3%95%E6%95%A3%E8%AB%96); the paragraph numbers are editorial and permit comparison with the Modern Chinese version. Obvious corruptions are emended only where necessary, while unresolved readings are identified in the notes. The closing “A chance note from the fifth month of the *yimao* year” dates only the final remark, not the compilation as a whole.
>
> **Translation note**: Technical vocabulary for script types, brushwork, and classical aesthetics is translated contextually rather than forced into modern typeface categories.

**1**　In writing small regular script, one must exert great force. Plant the brush against the paper as though bracing an iron staff weighing a thousand *jin* against the ground.[^en-1] If you suppose that small characters require no weight of force—that they may be completed by flitting here and there, dotting and embellishing—then you are talking in your sleep about this art. Copy the *Huangting jing* several thousand times over; then work with a round-tipped brush and the strength of a fragrant elephant, move the wrist with utter concentration, and have shoulder and back convey the force of sinew and bone. Only after this force has long been welling up from the right Tianzhu may one speak of the transformations between the unorthodox and the orthodox.[^en-2]

**2**　In small regular script, the *zoubo*—the sweeping stroke associated with 辵/辶—is not difficult; the *leluo*, the checked settling of the stroke, is especially difficult. It is difficult to cut into stone as well. Those who write model calligraphy and those who engrave it alike commonly let this pass.[^en-3]

**3**　Writing characters consists simply in not letting oneself run riot. Stroke by stroke, line by line: make them even and secure. If the structure will pass, what could fail to work? Jingguang loved calligraphy and preserved several sheets by Wu Bafu; all had been written in moments of apprehensive diligence and care. Alas, he is dead. Jingguang studied this brush method closely and became the blue that surpasses the indigo.[^en-4]

**4**　There are no ingenious tricks in writing characters; there is only upright awkwardness. Carry uprightness to its extreme and the marvelous is born: in the end, this is no more than “great skill appears clumsy.” If you do not believe it, then, just as the brush descends, first conceive an intention: “I shall make this character assume such-and-such a configuration.” Once the character is complete, its structure will be utterly at odds with your intention. From this too one may know that the natural seam within it cannot be fabricated. “Ability lies in a practiced hand”—a homely saying has disclosed the Way. Before the age of forty, Wang Duo strained every sinew to contrive his characters; after forty, they fell into measure without intention, and he thereby became a master.

**5**　The Jin is the Jin; the Six Dynasties, the Six Dynasties; the Tang, the Tang; the Song, the Song; the Yuan, the Yuan. Of late, perfectly good brush methods have been written to ruin by a single house: Jin that is not Jin, Six Dynasties that are not Six Dynasties, Tang that is not Tang, Song and Yuan that are neither Song nor Yuan. And still it glitters and preens in its prettiness, imagining itself the grand culmination of them all. Anyone with eyes sees at a glance its taste for the inner apartments.

**6**　Tang Lin remarks: “This is said of Dong Wenmin,” that is, Dong Qichang.[^en-5]

**7**　I once detested Zhao Ziang. Holding the man cheap, I came to hate his calligraphy. Recently, however, I have looked at it closely, and it does not deserve wholesale condemnation. Its consummate ingratiation and lissome grace are naturally a low manner; yet its moist elegance and rounded fluency still belong to the orthodox lineage. It arose through a slight internal transformation of the *Lanting*. That it rises or falls with its age also follows the fortunes of *qi*: literary composition is not alone in this.

**8**　I know full well calligraphy’s finest realm. When at first I want it to be thus but cannot make it thus, this is because mind and hand, paper and brush—subject and object—are mutually at odds. To intend it thus and be able to make it thus: that is craft. Not to intend it thus and yet have it become thus: that is Heaven. Each line has the Heaven of that line; each character has the Heaven of that character. Spirit arrives and the brush arrives: Heaven. The brush does not arrive, yet spirit arrives: Heaven. Arrival and non-arrival—nothing is not Heaven. What more can I say? It is, after all, difficult to speak of.

**9**　If regular script does not come from seal script, clerical script, and *bafen*, it is merely a slave’s posture and not worth looking at. Old Suo Jing already grasped this principle; look at the *Jijiu zhang* and it becomes abundantly clear. What are called seal, clerical, and *bafen* scripts must not be considered only in their outward forms: everything turns on the liveliness of the brush as it moves, turns, and folds. Vulgar characters are laid out entirely by human exertion, and precisely through such arranging and settling they lose the marvel of nature’s own workings. Examine how the brush descends in ancient seal and clerical script: one cannot tell at all how the forms were laid out. They seem greatly scattered and disordered, yet in the end one could not rearrange them on their behalf. Unless writing reaches transformation, its wonder does not appear; yet how could transformation be easily attained? Unless one enters through correctness, one cannot emerge in transformation. If only one can enter correctly, the debased air of maidservant and rustic vulgarity will disappear of itself. Yet an unpracticed brush is unresponsive, while one must also shun irreverent overfamiliarity; practiced facility itself verges upon such overfamiliarity. “Let the intention be upright and the body straight”: calligraphy communicates with archery. As for Yuanyang’s shooting—even Old Zhong, in the end, did not know it. This is the Way of avoiding overfamiliarity, and one must understand it.[^en-6]

**10**　At eight or nine I was already copying Yuanchang, Zhong You, but I did not resemble him. As I grew somewhat older, there was the *Huangting jing*, the *Cao E Stele*, the *Essay on Yue Yi*, the *Eulogy on Dongfang Shuo*, the *Thirteen Lines of the Rhapsody on the Goddess of the Luo*, and, farther down, the *Essay on Dispelling Error*: there was nothing I did not copy, yet not one copy came close. Finally I wrote after Duke Lu’s *Family Temple Stele* and gained a little of its disjointedness. Then, tracing the current upstream, I copied the *Dispute over Seating*, rather wishing to resemble it; advancing once more, I copied the *Lanting*. Although I did not obtain its spirit and expression, I gradually began to know the broad outlines of this art. In old age I can no longer write small regular script. Yet with the *Huangting*, I sharpen my sense of its minutiae day after day; just as I am about to set down the brush, it is once again a thousand *li* away.[^en-7]

**11**　Characters differ from literary composition in this: if a single stroke in a character does not resemble the ancients, it does not constitute a character; but if literary composition serves merely as a printing block for the ancients, ought it still to be called composition? The transformations and adjustments within this cannot be exhaustively told. It is hardest of all to discuss them with vulgar scholars.

**12**　What have written characters to do with human affairs? And yet I fear precisely that they may carry a servile and vulgar air. Only when one can be free of servile and vulgar habits may one speak of a bearing and aspiration that rise day by day—and not in characters alone.

**13**　If, in writing regular script, one does not understand the transformations of seal and clerical script, then however far one writes into the marvelous realm, the result remains a vulgar pattern. Everything immeasurable in Zhong You and Wang Xizhi was obtained from this very matter. This old man has truly seen through its ground; it is only that my disciplined practice has not reached perfect purity. Hence I cannot yet have heart and hand answer one another at will. But when I happen to fall into accord, there are places where I do not fall short of the ancients by the slightest fraction. And when my seal or clerical script comes off to my satisfaction, it is truly enough to make one cry out in astonishment: one feels that ancient and *zhou* script, regular, running, cursive, and clerical script are fundamentally without difference.[^en-8]

**14**　In regular and running script, nothing surpasses the *Lanting*; next below it comes the *Shengjiao xu*. Neither survives in a good exemplar. Yet if one must first obtain a good exemplar before taking up the practice of copying, would this Way not all but come to an end? Recent students of calligraphy mostly devote themselves to the *Shengjiao*, but every one of them is a maidservant impersonating her mistress. Beside the *Lanting*, the *Shengjiao* is already only a colt beneath the carriage shafts; how much more so those who build a house beneath a house—slaves twice enslaved? Zhao Ziang was adept at feeling his way into this principle and grasped its meaning. Yet he put too much running script into regular script, wholly unaware that the *Lanting* puts much regular script into its running script. Of the single record known as the *Lanting*, the Dingwu version is one of those most relished by the world; in my view, however, nothing surpasses the Tang copy on silk. This may be said to one who understands, but it is difficult to discuss with someone outside the gate. If judged by gross departures from the model, even Zijing, Wang Xianzhi, should not be studied—how much less anyone else. That the school of Mi Dian, Mi Fu, came into being was Zijing’s crime; that the school of the present day came into being is the Mi family’s crime. No: this is not the creators’ crime, but the fault of those who study them. Anyone with serious purpose will certainly not fall into this evil road. These are my reckless remarks; yet they have also received the assent of farsighted and discerning colleagues. I would not dare force others to agree with me.[^en-9]

**15**　In all things Heaven prevails; Heaven cannot be deceived. Man is wholly of Heaven. Yet if one has not trained through the human and deceives oneself by invoking Heaven, Heaven cannot be fabricated out of empty air. The human is Heaven’s agent. Draw it forth through diligence, and Heaven is not buried deep. The single Way of writing characters already contains this clue. Accumulate months and years, and you will know it for yourself.[^en-10]

**16**　Blear-eyed and impetuous fellows call sheer coarseness “heroic amplitude.” Coarseness is not amplitude. If it is truly heroic, it cannot be coarse. Tell me: in Master Mao, the Lord of the Secretariat—the writing brush—do we prize its coarseness, or do we choose it for its long, sharp point? If coarseness were what mattered, what difficulty would there be in twisting sheep or ox hair into brushes as thick as a finger, an arm, or a belly? Would those not serve perfectly well? Why then select hairs from a rabbit’s spine, a civet’s back, or a rat’s whiskers?[^en-11]

**17**　What is inconceivable in Han clerical script is simply its hardness and awkwardness. Originally it had no intention of calculated layout or measured fitting. In all the components—their left and right sides, breadth and narrowness, openness and density—the hand proceeds wherever it will: one unbroken current of nature’s workings. The presently circulating *Shenglin Liang Hu Stele* looks like an object cast in a brick mould and is utterly without savor. I do not know who traced and reproduced it. It is detestable in the extreme.[^en-12]

**18**　Those who do not know the origins of seal and *zhou* script, yet discourse on the study of characters and the art of calligraphy, are all asleep. They merely furnish the enlightened with a laugh.

**19**　Literary composition is a minor craft and has no exalted standing beside the Way; how much less this business of writing characters—what has it to do with the Way? My family has practiced it for six or seven generations in unbroken succession, yet none of them was made its servant for other people. Only with me began the misery of having to attend to vulgar creatures. They repeatedly force me to write before their very faces, imagining that this way they obtain the genuine article. Of the things I produce facing such people, not one is worth looking at. Resentment and vexation are already lodged within me, utterly violating the marvelous, self-achieved accord of mind and hand. How could outsiders possibly know the truth of it? Yet there is also an untransmitted secret here. Pretenders to understanding repeatedly claim that they can distinguish my calligraphy from my son’s; I can only cover my mouth at them. Broadly speaking, they take the work with heavy ink and an unbridled brush, filled with black, branching tangles, to be the father’s; work with light ink and a graceful brush, bright and lovely between the lines, they take to be the son’s. Whenever I hear their judgments, they are merely peddling their folly. Over the past two or three years, the brush that stood in for mine in fact most often came from my nephew Ren. People would immediately declare that it was genuinely my writing. They know of the son but not the nephew, who thus repeatedly saved me labor. Alas! Ren abruptly left me a year ago. Whenever I now receive a commission and take up the brush, my heart aches with grief. How could I ransom back this younger Ruan of mine? A chance note from the fifth month of the *yimao* year.[^en-13]


[^en-1]: Wikisource records a variant between 金 and 斤 in “千金／斤鐵杖.” The idiomatic and semantically cogent reading is “a thousand-*jin* iron staff.”

[^en-2]: The transmitted punctuation around “寫黃庭經數千過了，用圓鋒筆、香象力” is unstable. “Fragrant elephant” is a Buddhist figure for prodigious strength. “Right Tianzhu” probably denotes the Tianzhu region at the nape and shoulder, but the precise anatomical reference is not secure.

[^en-3]: 走波 has a strong variant 辵波, supporting its interpretation as a sweeping stroke associated with the 辵/辶 component. 勒 names a controlled horizontal stroke, but 勒落 and the final syntax remain uncertain; the translation therefore avoids assigning them an excessively precise operation.

[^en-4]: Jingguang and Wu Bafu cannot be securely identified. 蘭 in 青於蘭 is an evident error for 藍, from the saying “blue comes from indigo and surpasses it.” The identity of the person whose death is lamented is grammatically probable rather than certain.

[^en-5]: Dong Wenmin is Dong Qichang, whose posthumous name was Wenmin. “Tang Lin remarks” is a later editorial interjection, not part of Fu Shan’s own argument; Tang Lin’s identity remains uncertain.

[^en-6]: “Yuanyang’s shooting” and “Old Zhong” appear to be a private or textually damaged allusion and cannot presently be identified. The preceding phrase “intention upright, body straight” invokes classical discourse on ritual archery.

[^en-7]: Wikisource reads 朔, 曰, and 裁. The translation follows the contextually persuasive readings 溯, 日, and 纔: “trace upstream,” “daily,” and “just as.” The latter two remain conjectural.

[^en-8]: 阿堵 is a colloquial expression meaning “this very thing,” here the principle of transformation from seal and clerical scripts. Fu Shan’s claim that the scripts are “fundamentally without difference” concerns a shared principle of brushwork, not literal identity of historical forms.

[^en-9]: The *Shengjiao xu* is the text assembled by the Tang monk Huairen from characters attributed to Wang Xizhi. “Dingwu” denotes the celebrated Dingwu recension of the *Lanting*; “the Tang copy on silk” denotes a version traditionally regarded as a Tang tracing on silk. 抹索 is treated as a vernacular form close to 摸索, “to feel or grope one’s way into.”

[^en-10]: Wikisource’s “凡事天勝天，不可期人，純天矣” is syntactically incoherent. The translation follows an [independently attested reading](https://nhuir.nhu.edu.tw/bitstream/987654321/29040/1/110NHU00076004-001.pdf): “凡事天勝，天不可欺。人純天矣。” The passage expressly rejects invoking naturalness as a substitute for training.

[^en-11]: Wikisource reads 卯君中書 and 亦屬其銳而長. The wordplay and the allusion to Han Yu’s *Biography of Mao Ying* indicate 毛君／中書君, “Master Mao, Lord of the Secretariat,” a personification of the brush; 亦取其銳而長, “or choose it for being sharp and long,” is the probable underlying reading.

[^en-12]: Another witness reads 墼模, “brick mould,” clarifying Wikisource’s shorter 模. The title 聖林梁鵠碑 likely denotes a stele attributed to Liang Hu in the Confucian sacred precinct, but the exact transmitted exemplar is not certain.

[^en-13]: Fu Ren was Fu Shan’s nephew and sometimes wrote in his stead. “Younger Ruan” is an affectionate learned designation for a nephew, invoking Ruan Xian as Ruan Ji’s nephew. The *yimao* year is 1675; the date belongs to this final remark, not necessarily to the preceding eighteen remarks or to the later compilation as a whole.
